Ten divine invocations — purpose, practice & chanting guide.

The Significance of Mantras

The word mantra comes from Sanskrit — manas (mind) and tra (tool or instrument). A mantra is literally a tool for the mind. When chanted with intention, a mantra moves beyond language into vibration — it works at the level of sound, breath, and awareness to bring the mind into stillness and the heart into alignment.

Vibrational Healing

Every mantra carries a specific sound frequency that resonates with different energy centers in the body. Regular chanting activates these centers, releasing stored tension and restoring natural flow — physically, emotionally, and energetically.

Quieting the Mind

The repetition of a mantra gives the mind a single point of focus. Over time, the inner noise — the constant stream of thoughts, worries, and distractions — begins to settle. What remains is clarity, presence, and a deep sense of inner calm.

Rewiring Patterns

Chanting creates new neural pathways. When we return to the same mantra day after day, we are literally reshaping how the brain responds to stress, fear, and habit. Old patterns dissolve. New responses — rooted in awareness — take their place.

Connection to the Sacred

Each mantra is an invocation — a call to a quality within us and beyond us. Whether it is wisdom, strength, peace, or grace, chanting opens a channel between the personal self and something larger. It is devotion made audible.

Transformation through mantra is not sudden — it is steady. It works the way water shapes stone: not by force, but by return. Each chant is a return. Each session a deepening. Each day a quiet revolution in the way we meet ourselves and our lives.

How to Chant

01

Posture

Sit comfortably with your spine upright — on the floor, a cushion, or a chair. Keep the body relaxed but alert. Rest your hands on your knees, palms facing up.

02

Breath

Begin with three slow, deep breaths. Let the exhale be longer than the inhale. This signals to the nervous system that it is safe to settle.

03

Voice

You may chant aloud, in a whisper, or silently in the mind. Aloud is most powerful for beginners — the vibration in the body anchors attention. Silent chanting is the deepest form.

04

Direction

Face east at sunrise or north during the day. These directions are traditionally aligned with solar energy and magnetic flow — supporting clarity and receptivity.

05

Mala Beads

Hold the mala in your right hand. Use your thumb to move one bead per chant. When you reach the Guru bead (the large central bead), pause, reverse direction — do not cross it.

06

Intention

Before you begin, hold your intention quietly — healing, clarity, gratitude, release. The mantra carries your intention into the field of sound. Set it consciously.

Why 108

The number 108 is considered sacred across Vedic, Buddhist, and yogic traditions. A mala has 108 beads — one full round of chanting completes a cycle of 108 repetitions. This number is not arbitrary.

Astronomy

The distance between the Earth and the Sun is approximately 108 times the Sun's diameter. The distance between the Earth and the Moon is approximately 108 times the Moon's diameter. Ancient Vedic astronomers knew this.

The Body

There are 108 marma points — vital energy junctions — in the human body. There are 108 nadis (energy channels) said to converge at the heart center. Chanting 108 times touches each one.

Sanskrit

There are 54 letters in the Sanskrit alphabet. Each has a masculine (Shiva) and feminine (Shakti) form — 54 × 2 = 108. The entire field of sound, in all its polarity, is held within 108.

Completion

1 represents the singular — the source. 0 represents emptiness and wholeness. 8 represents infinity. Together: one being moving through wholeness toward the infinite. A complete cycle.

Best Time to Practice

Brahma Muhurta

4:00 – 6:00 AM

The most auspicious window of the day. The mind is naturally quiet, the air is still, and the veil between waking and the deeper self is thinnest. Even 15 minutes here carries the effect of an hour later in the day.

Sunrise

Dawn

As light enters the world, chanting aligns your energy with the day ahead. Surya mantras are especially powerful at this time. Face east and offer the practice to the rising sun.

Midday

12:00 PM

A natural pause point. Even a short round of chanting at noon resets the nervous system, clears mental fog, and reconnects you to intention when the day has pulled you in many directions.

Sunset

Dusk

The transition from day to night is a sacred threshold. Evening chanting releases what the day has accumulated — stress, reactivity, overstimulation — and prepares the inner ground for rest and renewal.

Your First Session

You do not need years of practice to begin. You need five minutes, a quiet space, and a willingness to show up. Here is a simple way to start today.

1

Choose one mantra

Start with Om Namah Shivaya or Om Shanti — both are gentle, accessible, and deeply calming. Do not try to learn all ten at once.

2

Find a quiet place

It does not need to be a dedicated altar. A corner of your room, a quiet chair, the floor by a window — anywhere you will not be interrupted for five to ten minutes.

3

Sit, breathe, begin

Close your eyes. Take three slow breaths. Then begin chanting — aloud or in a whisper. Let the sound be natural, not performative. There is no perfect way to sound.

4

Chant 11 or 21 times

For a first session, 11 rounds is enough. Use your fingers to count if you have no mala. The number matters less than the quality of your attention.

5

Sit in silence after

When you finish, do not immediately reach for your phone or stand up. Sit quietly for one or two minutes. Let the resonance settle. This is where the mantra does its deepest work.

6

Return tomorrow

Consistency is everything. One sincere session each day for 21 days creates a foundation. After 40 days, a practice becomes a part of who you are.

The most important thing is not how well you chant — it is that you chant. The mantra meets you where you are. Begin there.
